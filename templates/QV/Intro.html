What is Quadratic Voting?


QV is a method for decision making that allows you not only to express how you feel about a certain topic but also allows you to express how strongly you feel about it (i.e. How often you want to vote for it).
This solves an issue of majority voting, which assumes that everyone cares for an issue equally, which is rarely the case. The reality is that some voters do not care about certain issues, care moderately about others and care deeply about a few.


Why Quadratic ?


Each participant is given an amount of credits that can be used to vote for an issue. However, the cost of casting more than one vote for an issue is quadratic, not linear. So, the marginal cost of each additional vote is far higher than that of the previous vote.

Here is the Quadratic Voting formula: Cost to the voter = (Number of votes)^2

Imagine that a vote generally costs 1 credit to put toward an issue, and you have 100 voting credits. You want to cast your vote toward protecting endangered species. Casting one vote will cost you 1 credit. However, casting two votes for the same issue will cost you 4 credits, casting three votes for the same issue will cost you 9 and casting 10 votes for the same issue will cost you all of your credits.


Further information: Radical Markets: Uprooting Capitalism and Democracy for a Just Society, Posner & Weyl, Princton University Press, 2018


Today's experiment


This experiment is meant to analyze possible differences between majority voting and quadratic voting. Therefore, you will be asked to vote on 16 different questions covering a variety of issues. First, you will be asked to cast one voter per question that will be aggregated in a “traditional” majority voting system. Second, you will have the opportunity to vote in a quadratic system. Here, you can decide freely how many votes to cast per question, following the cost of voting formula introduced earlier (cost = votes^2). In this second, quadratic system you have a total of 20 credits. The voting pages will look like this:
